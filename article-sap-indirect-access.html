Audit Defence

SAP’s indirect access audits are quietly ramping again. Here’s what changed.

Two new clauses in the 2026 GTC have shifted the burden of proof on indirect use. We map the audit triggers, the documentation gaps that get exploited, and a 30-day baseline plan.

VP
Shubham Singh
PAS & SAM Helpdesk Expert | Assistant Manager
· 11 February 2026 · 8 min read

SAP indirect access went quiet for about three years. The 2022 conversation that lit up the SAP user community — the Diageo precedent, the Digital Access framework, the engine-document model — settled into a familiar shape, and most enterprises moved on to other licensing concerns.

The 2026 General Terms and Conditions have changed the temperature again. Two new clauses have shifted the burden of proof on indirect use, and SAP's License Audit Services team is using them. Across our last six audit-defence engagements, indirect access has been the central issue in five.

What changed in the 2026 GTC

Two clauses in particular are driving the shift.

Clause 6.4 — "Authorised Use Documentation"

The 2024 GTC required SAP to demonstrate that indirect use was occurring before triggering a Digital Access licence requirement. The 2026 wording reverses this: the customer is now obliged, on request, to produce documentation evidencing the categorisation of every system that connects to SAP — including categorisation of which connections are "humans" versus "non-human consumers" versus "engine-driven processes."

The shift is subtle but consequential. In an audit, the question is no longer "can SAP prove indirect use" but "can the customer prove the absence of unlicensed indirect use." The default assumption now sits with the auditor.

Clause 9.1 — "Documentation Refresh Cadence"

Authorised Use Documentation must now be "current within the most recent 12 months" at the time of any audit. In practice, this means an annual exercise. Many enterprises did the original Digital Access categorisation in 2019-2021, captured the result in a static spreadsheet, and never revisited it. Three years later that spreadsheet is no longer "current," even if the categorisations are still substantively correct.

The 2026 GTC didn't change what indirect access is. It changed who has to prove it. And the evidence threshold has moved from SAP's audit team to the customer's licence administrator.

The audit triggers we're seeing

From the engagements we've worked, three triggers are showing up consistently.

Trigger 1: A new SaaS or middleware connection

Any new connection from a non-SAP system into the ECC or S/4HANA estate — whether MuleSoft, Boomi, custom IDocs, or a SaaS integrator — is the most common audit trigger. The licensing team picks up the connection in their architecture review, asks for the Authorised Use Documentation, and the existence (or absence) of current categorisation determines what happens next.

Trigger 2: M&A activity

Any acquisition that brings new systems into the SAP-connected landscape will trigger a documentation request. The 2026 wording explicitly applies to "systems acquired through corporate transactions," which removes a previous grey area.

Trigger 3: An unrelated licence dispute

This is the one we see most often. A negotiation about something else — a HANA renewal, an S/4HANA conversion, a new RISE commitment — produces an attachment that re-evaluates indirect access at the same time. The new clauses make this easier for SAP to do.

The documentation gaps that get exploited

The Authorised Use Documentation is, in our experience, the difference between a 30-day audit and a six-month one. Three gaps appear consistently.

Gap 1: Engine-document categorisation drift

The Digital Access framework prices on document type and volume — sales documents, purchase documents, financial documents, manufacturing documents. The categorisation done in 2019 may not reflect the actual document mix being created in 2026, especially if the business has shifted toward more direct customer integration. SAP's auditors have access to the document creation tables; the customer's documentation often does not match.

Gap 2: Read-only access ambiguity

The 2026 GTC retains the principle that read-only access from non-SAP systems is generally not chargeable, but the documentation requirements around proving read-only-ness have tightened. Several clients have discovered that a "read-only" connection set up in 2018 has, through subsequent enhancement, gained write capability that was never re-licensed.

Gap 3: Service account proliferation

Service accounts are a perennial issue. Each non-named service account that connects to SAP is, technically, a non-human consumer that requires Digital Access licensing. The auditors have process discovery tools that identify these. The customer rarely has a current inventory.

A 30-day baseline plan

If you're reading this and your Authorised Use Documentation is older than 12 months, the following is the minimum work required to get current.

Week 1: System inventory

Catalogue every system that connects to SAP. Include its purpose, integration mechanism (RFC, BAPI, IDoc, OData, REST), data flow direction, and current ownership. The objective is a single source of truth for "what touches SAP from outside."

Week 2: Categorisation

For each connection, categorise the consumers as Named User (human), Digital Access (non-human document creator), or Engine (system-driven process). Where the categorisation is ambiguous, document the reasoning. Ambiguity itself is acceptable; missing reasoning is not.

Week 3: Volumetrics

For Digital Access categorisations, pull the actual document creation volumes from the relevant tables for the most recent 12-month window. Compare against the licensed entitlement. Identify any over-consumption before SAP does.

Week 4: Remediation and documentation

Where over-consumption exists, decide between true-up purchase, architectural remediation (re-routing creation through licensed Named Users), or commercial negotiation. Document the position taken, with evidence, in a format that meets the GTC's "current" requirement.

Practitioner's Takeaway

The 2026 GTC has shifted the documentation burden. Move with it.

  • Refresh your Authorised Use Documentation annually. The 12-month currency requirement is enforceable.
  • Maintain a live inventory of every non-SAP system that connects to your SAP estate, with categorisation and ownership.
  • Service accounts are the single most overlooked source of unlicensed indirect access. Inventory them now.
  • If an audit notice arrives, do not respond on auto-pilot. The first 72 hours of an SAP audit shape the next 90 days.

The shorter version

SAP didn't make indirect access more aggressive. They made the documentation requirement more aggressive. For most enterprises, the licensing position is defensible — provided the documentation supports it. Most documentation, right now, doesn't.
