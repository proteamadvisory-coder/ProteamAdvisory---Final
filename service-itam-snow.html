The problem we solve

Snow is changing. So is the question of when to migrate.

Snow Software has been one of the most widely deployed SAM platforms globally for over a decade — particularly strong on user-centric SAM, application metering, and SaaS discovery. The 2022 acquisition by Flexera changed the strategic trajectory. Snow Licence Manager remains supported, and Snow Atlas continues to evolve. But the long-term consolidation onto Flexera One is now visible on the roadmap.

That creates two distinct sets of questions. For those staying — how do we get the most from Snow Licence Manager and Snow Atlas right now? Connector tuning, SRL management, software model maintenance, integration with the wider IT estate. For those migrating — what's the right sequence, what data carries over, what gets rebuilt in Flexera One, and how do we run both in parallel during the transition?

Vendor-independent. No reseller agreement with Flexera or Snow. The advice is grounded in your interests, not the platform vendor's renewal calendar.

What's included

Six workstreams. Whatever your Snow future looks like.

Whether you're staying on Snow, optimising your current deployment, or migrating onto Flexera One — engagements cover these areas.

// 01

Inventory connector tuning

Configuration and tuning of Snow Inventory connectors — agents, agentless discovery, virtualisation source integration. The platform's accuracy depends entirely on the input data, and Snow connectors reward careful configuration.

// 02

Software recognition library management

SRL tuning, custom application records, suite identification, and ongoing curation. Production-grade Snow deployments run on tuned SRL, maintained against your specific publisher portfolio.

// 03

Licence model & contract load

Loading your contractual position into Snow at the level of granularity the platform needs to produce defensible compliance positions — swap rights, downgrade rights, sub-capacity rules, geographic restrictions.

// 04

Snow Atlas migration & adoption

For clients moving from Snow Licence Manager to Snow Atlas — structured migration support: data mapping, custom report rebuilding, integration recreation, user training. Atlas is not a like-for-like upgrade; we de-risk the transition.

// 05

Flexera One migration support

For clients consolidating onto Flexera One — structured migration: what data carries over, what gets rebuilt from scratch, the parallel-run period design, and the cutover sequencing. Typically a 4–9 month programme.

// 06

Reporting & operating model

Designing the operational reports, schedule, and review cadences that turn the Snow deployment into an actively useful governance system — not a data lake the SAM team peers into occasionally.

How we work

Four stages. Whichever direction you choose.

Optimisation engagements run 6–14 weeks. Flexera One migrations run 4–9 months depending on estate complexity.

01

Discover & scope

Free consultation. We understand your current Snow deployment, the relationship with your Snow account team, your strategic intent (stay, optimise, or migrate), and your timeline pressures. Scope agreed on that basis.

02

Diagnose & design

Structured diagnostic of the current deployment — connectors, SRL, licence model, reporting. For migrations, additional data-mapping and dependency analysis on the Flexera One target state.

03

Implement & optimise

Execution. Connector tuning, SRL curation, licence model rebuild, or migration cut-over. Two-week sprints with weekly progress reporting and clear sprint acceptance criteria.

04

Stabilise & hand over

Knowledge transfer, runbook documentation, 30/60/90-day hyper-care. For migrations, structured cut-over governance and parallel-run reconciliation.

Licence visibility

From fragmented data to clear positions.

Snow's core strength is user-centric SAM — but that only delivers if the underlying data is complete. Scattered inventory signals, an un-tuned SRL, and under-loaded contract data leave the platform producing positions you can't trust or defend.

Our work crystallises that fragmented data into a clean, defensible compliance position — one the SAM team can actually use in publisher conversations.
