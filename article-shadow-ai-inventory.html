SaaS Management

The Shadow AI inventory: a 2026 audit playbook for CIOs.

Browser-based AI tools, departmental Cursor seats, embedded agent licences inside SaaS contracts you already own. A four-step discovery sweep that takes a week and finds 2-3x what your CASB sees.

VP
Vinod Patel
PAS & SAM Helpdesk Expert | Assistant Manager
· 14 January 2026 · 6 min read

The phrase "Shadow IT" has been with us since the cloud era began. Shadow AI is the same problem with a faster compounding rate. In the last 12 months we've sat in 14 enterprise discovery exercises where the AI inventory the IT team produced was between 30% and 60% smaller than the inventory we were able to surface in a four-step sweep.

For CIOs preparing for the wave of AI governance audits — internal, regulatory, and from the board — knowing what's actually in the estate matters more than ever. This is the discovery sweep that consistently finds the missing 30-60%.

Why the standard inventory misses so much

Most enterprise AI inventories rely on three sources: the procurement record, the SSO directory, and the CASB or DLP feed. All three are necessary. None of them is sufficient.

Procurement records capture the AI tools that came through formal purchasing — typically the larger commitments. They miss the per-seat tools paid on individual corporate cards, the free-tier accounts that haven't yet hit a paywall, and the AI capabilities that came embedded inside SaaS contracts already on the books.

The SSO directory captures tools that have been integrated with corporate identity. Free-tier and personal-account-based AI tools rarely qualify. Browser-based tools used without authentication are entirely invisible.

The CASB or DLP feed captures network traffic to known AI domains. It typically lags the rate at which new tools appear (a tool that launched last month likely isn't in the CASB ruleset yet) and misses anything routed through general-purpose cloud services.

The four-step sweep

The sweep takes a working week with a small team and produces an inventory that is materially more complete than any single source provides.

Step 1: Corporate card analysis

Pull 18 months of corporate card transactions, filtered to recurring charges below the procurement-approval threshold. Sort by merchant. The list of merchant names that appear here is, in most enterprises, the largest single source of "Shadow AI" — individual or departmental subscriptions to tools like Cursor, Perplexity Pro, ChatGPT Team, Notion AI, Otter, Fathom, Loom AI, Granola, Read.ai, and dozens of vertical-specific AI tools.

The merchants are usually self-evident; categorisation takes a few hours. The output is a list of "AI tools the enterprise is paying for that nobody at the centre knew about." In one recent engagement, this step alone surfaced 47 distinct vendors and approximately $340,000 of annual spend.

Step 2: Browser telemetry sweep

If your endpoint management deploys a browser fleet (Edge for Business, Chrome Enterprise), enable browser telemetry against a curated list of AI-tool domains for a 30-day window. The list should include the obvious entrants (chat.openai.com, claude.ai, gemini.google.com, copilot.microsoft.com) and the long tail (notion.so/ai, cursor.com, perplexity.ai, you.com, character.ai, replit.com, midjourney.com, runwayml.com, pika.art, plus the 80-100 second-tier tools that appear in most enterprise traffic).

The telemetry produces both a usage pattern (how often, by which department) and an exposure profile (which users are routing potentially sensitive corporate context into which tools). For audit and risk purposes, the exposure profile matters more than the volume figure.

Step 3: Embedded-AI contract sweep

The least visible category of AI tooling is the AI features embedded in SaaS contracts the enterprise has already signed. ServiceNow Now Assist, Salesforce Einstein Copilot, Microsoft 365 Copilot, GitHub Copilot, Workday Illuminate, Atlassian Intelligence, Slack AI — every major SaaS platform now ships AI features either bundled or as a paid add-on. Many of these are licensed on terms that change consumption-based pricing.

The sweep here is to pull every active SaaS contract above a meaningful threshold and audit the AI inclusions: which features are bundled, which are paid add-ons, what the consumption metering looks like, and what the enterprise has actually enabled. The gap between "what we're contractually licensed for" and "what we have switched on" is itself useful information for governance.

Step 4: Repository and code-context audit

For technical AI tools — coding assistants in particular — the missing inventory is usually in the engineering organisation. GitHub Copilot, Cursor, Claude Code, Codeium, Tabnine, Continue.dev, and a growing list of smaller tools all have varying degrees of corporate visibility.

The audit looks at three artefacts: editor configurations checked into repositories, IDE plugin manifests on developer endpoints (where MDM access permits), and the corporate package manager logs for AI-related dependencies. The output is a picture of which AI coding tools are actually in active developer workflows, regardless of whether they were procured centrally.

The Shadow AI inventory question isn't whether your engineers are using AI. They are. The question is whether the corporate context they're feeding into AI tools is going somewhere your governance posture says is acceptable.

What to do with the inventory once you have it

The discovery is the easy part. Three governance moves usually follow.

Consolidation where it makes sense

Three different teams paying for three overlapping AI tools is wasted spend. The inventory makes the consolidation conversation possible — and consolidation onto an enterprise contract usually buys both better pricing and meaningfully better governance posture (data handling commitments, audit logs, SSO integration).

Approved-list governance

Rather than blocking everything that isn't on the corporate list, the high-functioning enterprises run an approved-list model: tools that have been through a lightweight risk review can be reimbursed; tools that haven't, can't. The reimbursement gate is the enforcement mechanism, not network blocks.

Annual refresh cadence

The Shadow AI inventory ages quickly. New tools appear monthly. The four-step sweep run annually keeps the picture current; run quarterly for sectors where the AI tooling pace is fastest (engineering organisations, marketing, knowledge-work professional services).

The CIO's audit-ready position

For an internal or regulatory AI audit, three artefacts demonstrate a credible governance posture.

The inventory itself, dated, with sources documented (procurement, SSO, browser telemetry, contracts, repositories). A current, sourced inventory is the foundation of every other governance conversation.

The approved-list policy, with its risk review criteria, its decision history, and its exceptions register. Auditors look for evidence of consistent application; intent matters less than evidence.

The data-flow categorisation: for each AI tool in the inventory, what categories of corporate data are reaching it, under what data handling commitments, with what residency. This is the artefact most enterprises don't have and most auditors increasingly ask for.

Practitioner's Takeaway

You can't govern what you can't see. The four-step sweep makes the estate visible.

  • Corporate card analysis surfaces departmental subscriptions; browser telemetry surfaces individual usage; contract sweep surfaces embedded AI; repository audit surfaces engineering tooling.
  • Run the sweep before the audit, not after. The artefacts you need for a credible governance posture take a week to produce — but only if you start before someone asks.
  • Consolidation is usually the financial case. Governance is usually the regulatory case. The inventory enables both.
  • Re-run annually at minimum. Quarterly in fast-moving organisations.

The shorter version

The Shadow AI inventory most enterprises maintain is missing the largest, fastest-growing portion of the estate. The four-step sweep is a week's work and produces an inventory that holds up under audit. Run it now; the next governance conversation will be about something else, but it will assume this one has been done.
