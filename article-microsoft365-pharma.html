Case Scenario

How a global pharma client cut Microsoft 365 spend by 34% without losing a single user.

An E5-to-E3 rebalance, a reclaim of 2,800 dormant licences, and a renegotiated Frontline tier — without touching power-user productivity. The exact decision tree we used.

VP
Shubham Singh
PAS & SAM Helpdesk Expert | Assistant Manager
· 02 March 2026 · 6 min read

The pharma client came to us in a familiar position. Microsoft 365 had become the second-largest software line item in their annual budget — 18,400 seats across an E5 estate that had grown organically through three years of pandemic-era expansion. The CFO's brief was simple: take 30% out, but don't degrade the experience for anyone who actually uses what they're paying for.

Eight weeks later we'd delivered a 34% reduction with zero user-impact tickets. The work wasn't clever. It was disciplined. This is the decision tree we walked.

Step one: surface the dormant estate

The first finding always surprises clients. We pulled the Microsoft 365 admin centre activity reports for a rolling 90-day window and cross-referenced against assigned licences. The result: 2,847 E5 licences had not registered a single sign-in event in the period. Of those, 1,680 belonged to leavers, contractors past end-of-engagement, or test accounts that had never been deactivated.

This isn't a Microsoft problem; it's a HR-to-IT data flow problem. Joiner-mover-leaver processes work for badge access. They almost never work for SaaS licences. The reclaim itself takes hours. Building the workflow that prevents the same accumulation in 18 months takes longer — and matters more.

The reclaim mechanics

Reclaiming dormant E5 seats is straightforward in Microsoft's licensing terms — but only at renewal, not mid-term, unless you have a Microsoft Customer Agreement (MCA) with True-Down provisions. Our client had a standard EA, so we mapped the dormancy data to the renewal date and timed the count adjustment with their commercial team. Reclaim value: $1.8M annually at list, $1.4M after their EA discount.

Step two: the E5-to-E3 rebalance

The second move is the one that makes finance teams nervous and IT teams resistant. E5 includes Microsoft 365 Defender, Power BI Pro, and the advanced security stack. E3 doesn't. Moving anyone "down" is politically charged.

The data is unambiguous, though. Across the client's 18,400 active users, only 4,100 had used any E5-exclusive feature in the previous 90 days. Of those, 2,300 were Power BI Pro users — a feature available standalone for less than the E5 differential. The remaining 1,800 were genuine power users of Defender for Office 365 (Plan 2) and the security suite.

The cost of an unused E5 licence is not the licence price minus E3. It's the licence price minus E3 plus the standalone Power BI Pro you'd buy back for the user who actually needs it. Run the full math.

The actual rebalance

Final mix after the rebalance:

  • 1,800 E5 licences for genuine power users (security operations, BI architects, executives with Defender mailbox protection requirements)
  • 10,400 E3 licences for general knowledge workers
  • 2,300 E3 + Power BI Pro standalone bundles for analysts
  • 1,063 Microsoft 365 F3 (Frontline) for warehouse, lab tech, and field staff with kiosk-style usage
  • 0 dormant seats on the renewal day

Step three: the Frontline tier renegotiation

The Frontline conversation is often the most overlooked. Microsoft's F3 SKU is licensed at roughly a quarter of E3 list price and is contractually appropriate for "workers who do not require a dedicated computer." For a pharma client, that includes warehouse pickers, lab technicians, plant operators, and field reps who use kiosk-shared devices.

The catch: F3 has a 1GB Exchange mailbox limit and excludes desktop Office. Either of those can be a deal-breaker for a particular user; both together rule out about a third of frontline candidates. But for the 1,063 employees who genuinely fit the profile, the migration was uncontroversial — and the savings real.

What the rebalance saved

Cumulative annual licence-only savings, before applying the renewed EA discount: $5.3M. After discount: roughly $4.2M. Against the original ~$12.4M effective annual M365 spend, that's the 34% the CFO had asked for — without anyone losing capability they were actually using.

What we'd do differently next time

Looking back across this engagement and four similar ones since, three things would have made the project even cleaner.

Run the dormancy sweep before the renewal date is on the calendar

Most enterprises start the optimisation conversation 90 days before their EA renews. By then, Microsoft's account team is already calculating commission against the existing run-rate. Starting 12 months ahead — when nobody is on a renewal clock — produces softer pricing and a less defensive customer-facing team.

Use Power BI Premium per User as a fork in the road

If your Power BI Pro user count is above 4,000, the Premium per User SKU starts to compete favourably with E5 inclusion. The breakpoint is mostly about whether you also need the Premium capacity features (paginated reports, larger model sizes). Run that math separately.

Document the rebalance for the next renewal

The single most valuable artefact we leave behind is a "user persona by SKU" map. Three years from now, when the next CFO asks why the M365 line is what it is, the answer needs to be readable in 10 minutes. We learnt this the expensive way on a different client whose 2022 rebalance had no documentation, and which had quietly drifted back toward E5-by-default by 2024.

Practitioner's Takeaway

The Microsoft 365 conversation is rarely about Microsoft 365.

  • The biggest savings live in the joiner-mover-leaver workflow, not in negotiating with the account team.
  • E5-to-E3 rebalancing only works if you do the standalone math (Power BI Pro, Defender, etc.) for the users who actually need those features.
  • F3 is genuinely valuable, but only for the 5-10% of any enterprise that fits the contractual definition.
  • The real prize is the workflow that prevents the same accumulation happening again.

The shorter version

$4.2M out, no user impact, eight weeks of work. None of it required a clever trick. All of it required the discipline to run the dormancy report, do the persona math, and have an honest conversation with the IT director about which of his executives actually used Defender for Office 365.

The clients who get this right are the ones who treat M365 the way they'd treat any other big-ticket recurring spend — with a baseline, an owner, and a rebalance cycle every 12 months.
